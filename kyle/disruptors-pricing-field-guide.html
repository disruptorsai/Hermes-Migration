Pricing Field Guide
for Disruptors.

Four rules govern every pricing conversation. Read them, internalize them, and let them shape every quote you put in front of a prospect. The numbers in this guide are floors, never ceilings — and the plan, not the tier, is what dictates the price.

01
Rule One
Floors, not ceilings.
You can charge whatever you can sell — as long as it does not drop below the stated minimum. If you can sell higher, sell higher. Disruptors with stronger close skills earn the right to bigger deals. Never apologize for a number.
02
Rule Two
No one-time builds. Ever.
Every website, chatbot, automation, and custom app is delivered inside an active retainer. One-time builds always create downstream issues — broken handoffs, scope drift, no one accountable. We sell the relationship, not the deliverable.
03
Rule Three
Sell the relationship.
You are not a vendor — you are their Chief AI Officer. Build speed, channel coverage, and team access scale with retainer size. After a build wraps, the client downgrades to management — but the retainer continues.
04
Rule Four
Plan-led pricing.
Build the plan to the prospect's needs first. The plan dictates the price — never the other way around. Our internal tier ladder is how we think about scaling fulfillment; we rarely mention it on calls. What clients see is a custom plan, three ways.

From lead to proposal — every deal.

Five steps from first touch to signed proposal. Run this sequence on every prospect — skipping a step is how deals get mispriced.

01Step
Assess company success
Before you say a number, get a feel for what the prospect can actually afford. Read the public signals first.
  • Are they "killing it" in their industry?
  • Employee count — proxy for scale and operational maturity.
  • Volume of reviews and social proof — proxy for revenue velocity.
  • Years in business — proxy for stability and budget durability.
02Step
Conduct market research
Use the marketing research assistant to identify which channels will be most powerful for their lead acquisition and sales. Based on the output, develop a loose idea of which items from our services list they should utilize.
03Step
Discovery & revenue analysis
On the call, get to the truth. The plan you build needs to fit the actual reality of their business.
  • What they're currently doing — and what they're not.
  • What assets they already have in place.
  • Their current revenue markers — how much, how stable, how growing.
04Step
Set the budget
Plan against 10–20% of monthly revenue, depending on how aggressively the prospect wants to market and grow. That figure is their marketing budget — and it's generally where pricing should land.
How much can you reasonably allocate toward your marketing and AI infrastructure, knowing it will save you [X] amount of time and likely make you [Y] amount of money?
05Step
Custom proposal · three options
Present the plan as a custom proposal with three tiered options. Anchor pricing technique — the structure of the choices does half the selling for you.
Option A · High
Anchor
Priced well above target. Pure anchor — establishes the upper bound and makes the recommended option feel reasonable by comparison.
Option B · Mid
The Plan
Everything the client actually wants and needs. This is the option built around the plan you developed in Steps 1–4. This is the one they pick.
Option C · Low
Decoy
Priced lower but missing something the client wants or needs. Its job is to make the recommended option more attractive — not to be picked.
i
Pricing Autonomy
The Disruptor running the deal has full pricing authority — above the floor. You don't need approval to charge $4,200 for a CAIO retainer if the prospect is a strong fit. You do need approval to charge $1,200. Floors are floors.
Section 02 · Pricing Guidelines

Plan first. Price follows.

We don't sell tiers — we sell plans built around what the client actually needs. These are the simple guidelines that govern where the price lands. Hold these four in your head; everything else is negotiable scope.

How to anchor a call
Don't open with a price or a tier. Open with the plan you're building. "Based on what you've told me, the right plan probably involves [X] and [Y]. Let me get a few more details and I'll come back with a custom proposal." The plan is the artifact — the price is just what the plan costs.

Plan-shaping heuristics.

When you're sketching the plan in Steps 1–4, these heuristics translate scope into a sensible price band.

  • One channel, lean budget → ~$850/mo. Real fulfillment, narrow scope. Don't undersell — this is a working program.
  • "We need help on multiple things" → $1,500–$3,500/mo, depending on whether they want execution-only or strategic direction.
  • "We need a marketing leader" → $3,500+. The Chief AI Officer / fractional CMO positioning starts here.
  • Custom builds in the picture (chatbot, voice agent, automation factory) → $3,500 minimum, more if the build is significant. Build speed scales with retainer size.
  • Multi-location or franchise → $5,000+. They need bandwidth, not just strategy.

Section 04 · Builds & Custom Work

Why we don't sell one-time builds.

Every build — websites, chatbots, voice agents, automations, custom apps — is delivered inside an active retainer. This isn't a billing trick. It's the only model that produces work clients are still happy with six months later.

Reason 01
One-time builds break.
Software rots. APIs deprecate. The site that launched in March will need a fix in May. Without a retainer, the client gets stuck with a deliverable nobody owns.
Reason 02
The relationship is the product.
We're selling Chief AI Officer, not a Webflow build. The build is what that relationship produces in month one. The retainer is what makes the build worth having.
Reason 03
It compounds for them.
A build inside a retainer keeps improving — the chatbot gets smarter, the funnel gets tuned, the automations expand. A one-time build peaks on day one and decays from there.

When a prospect wants a website, chatbot, or automation, build speed is determined by the retainer size. More retainer = more hours per month dedicated to the build = faster delivery. Use this to upsell scope when timeline matters.

Custom automation pricing flow.

When a prospect asks for something non-standard — a custom workflow, a voice agent, a multi-platform automation — never quote on the call. Run the flow:

Step 01
Capture the full SOW
Inputs, outputs, integrations, edge cases, expected volume. Detail matters — vague SOWs produce bad estimates.
Step 02
Send to fulfillment
Fulfillment returns a conservative estimate — always biased toward "more time, more buffer."
Step 03
Quote inside a retainer
Convert the estimate into retainer hours. Recommend the tier that makes the build comfortable, not tight.
Step 04
Set the downgrade path
Tell the client up front: after the build wraps, they can drop to management + updates. The retainer continues, the price comes down.
✓
The Downgrade Path
After a build is complete, the client can downgrade to a management and updates retainer — but the retainer relationship continues. Mention this on the sales call. It de-risks the bigger initial commitment by giving the prospect a clear "step-down" once the heavy lift is done.

Use the math, don't lead with it
Don't open the call with hourly breakdowns — it commodifies the relationship. Use this only when a prospect challenges the price or asks for justification. Then walk them through it confidently. The math is a defense weapon, not an opener.

Allocation heuristics.

  • CAIO hours go up when the client is in build mode (new website, new automation, new chatbot) or at a strategic inflection point — pricing, positioning, growth pivots.
  • VA hours go up when the client is in steady-state mode (ongoing posting, content production, blog cadence, ad ops).
  • If a client at $3,500 constantly demands CAIO time, that's a signal to scope the plan up to $5,000+ — they need more strategic bandwidth, and you'll burn margin if you don't move them up.
  • If a client mostly needs production work, the VA-heavy mix is fine and the margins are healthy — don't over-engineer the plan.
Section 06 · Objection Handling

What to say when they say…

The most common pricing objections, with the move that handles each one. Memorize the scripted responses — they're battle-tested. Adapt the tone to match your style, but keep the structure.

Can I just buy a website? I don't want a retainer.
We don't sell one-time builds — and the reason is selfish on your behalf. Every one-time website we ever built ended in a frustrated client three months later when something needed to change and there was nobody to call. Building inside a retainer means the site keeps improving, not decaying.
"We've been there. Selling you a website by itself is the fastest way to lose you as a client by month four. The retainer isn't extra — it's what makes the build worth having."
Why 10% on top of ad spend?
The 10% covers daily monitoring, creative iteration, platform reconciliation, and the operational overhead of running ad accounts well. It's the standard across every serious paid media agency — and we apply it without exception so the math is always clean.
"It's how every real ad agency operates. The 10% is what pays for the daily eyes on the account — without that, you're just running ads and hoping. We don't run ads on hope."
$850 feels low — what's the catch?
No catch. $850 is the floor for a single-channel program — real fulfillment, one channel, executed properly. It's where solo operators and early-stage businesses start. If you want multi-channel coverage or a fractional CMO, the plan would scope up to $1,500–$3,500 — that's where most of our clients land.
"$850 is our floor for one channel done well. If you want me thinking about your business across channels, the plan scopes up to fractional Chief AI Officer territory at $3,500."
$3,500/month is a lot. What am I actually getting?
Roughly 35 hours of blended team time per month — strategy, execution, and fulfillment — directed wherever you need it. Compare that to a single in-house marketer at ~$8K fully loaded, and you have access to a multi-disciplinary team for less than half. Plus a Chief AI Officer-level relationship.
"At $3,500, you're getting roughly 35 team hours a month, plus me as your fractional Chief AI Officer. The cheapest in-house marketing hire would run you double, and they'd only know one channel."
Can we negotiate the price down?
The numbers we share are floors, not opening positions. We can reshape the plan — fewer channels, lighter execution, smaller program — but we don't drop below the documented service floors. If $1,500 is too steep, the plan probably needs to scope down to a single-channel program at $850.
"I can absolutely scope it differently — but the floors are floors. If $1,500 is the wrong number, let's reshape the plan around a smaller program."
What happens after the website / chatbot is built?
You can downgrade to a management + updates retainer — same relationship, lower monthly. The retainer continues so we keep the thing healthy, but the heavy build hours roll off. Most clients stay with us indefinitely on the management tier.
"Once the build wraps, you don't keep paying build prices. You step down to a management retainer — the relationship continues, the number comes down."
My friend got [comparable service] for $500 cheaper.
They probably did. The question isn't who's cheapest — it's who's still going to be your team in eighteen months. Our pricing reflects what it actually costs to deliver real fulfillment with senior oversight. Anyone underbidding us is either subsidizing the work or cutting corners we won't cut.
"You can always find cheaper. The real question is whether the cheaper option is still going to be there when the wheels come off. We've watched a lot of those agencies disappear."
Can you build me a custom automation as a one-off?
No — and the reason is the same as websites. Custom automations need ongoing maintenance as APIs change, edge cases surface, and your business evolves. We scope the build, deliver it inside a retainer, and you can downgrade to management afterward.
"Custom automations are like custom code — they need a backstop. The retainer is the backstop. Without it, you're one API change away from a broken workflow and nobody to call."
I just need help with one thing — do I really need a retainer?
If it's truly one-off and tactical, you probably don't need us — and we'd tell you that. But if it's the kind of thing that needs to keep working, evolve, or scale, the retainer is what makes that possible. We don't take work we know we can't stand behind.
"If it's truly one-and-done, I'd send you somewhere else honestly. We only take work we can stand behind, and standing behind work means the retainer."
