Part 1: The Clean-Path Script

Call Flow at a Glance

Your goal is not to sell. It is to generate genuine interest and book a longer video conversation with Kyle.

1. Confirm Decision Maker
Is this [First Name]?
2. Introduce Yourself
Name + clear reason for the call
3. Ask for Permission
Do you have a couple minutes?
4. Frame the Two Ways
Replicate competitors + amplify what works
5. Find the Bottleneck
Walk me through the process
6. Suggest a Loose Fit
Directional suggestion + case study
7. Ask About Competitors
What do you see working for them?
8. Connect the Dots
AI can replicate proven plays
9. Soft Recommendation
Honest take + ask for video call
10. Confirm & Hand Off
Lock time, loop in Kyle
Steps 1–3

Open the Call

Step 01 — Confirm the Decision Maker
Hey, is this [First Name]?
Stay calm and friendly. You are not in a hurry. If someone else answers, switch to gatekeeper branch.
Step 02 — Introduce Yourself
Cool. My name is [Your Name]. I reached out because I'm confident I can make you money and save you time with my skill set in artificial intelligence and building systems around it.
Keep the tone grounded and confident, not hypey. The word "confident" does the work. Do not oversell past it.
Step 03 — Ask for Permission
That being said, I wanted to get to know you and your business quickly to see if a longer conversation would be worth exploring for both of us. Do you have a couple of minutes for me to ask what you've got going on?
If yes: continue. If no, maybe, or any resistance: go to Smokescreens (Part 3).
Steps 4–5

Frame AI & Find the Bottleneck

Step 04 — Frame the Two Ways AI Helps
There are really two ways we help businesses with AI. The first is, we identify what your most successful direct competitors are already doing and use AI to help you replicate that strategy faster. The second is, we look at what's already working for you and use AI to help you do ten times more of it, in less time.

So let me ask you this: what's currently the most effective strategy you're using to bring in sales?
This frame positions you as a strategist, not a salesperson. Shut up and listen. Whatever they name is the thread you pull for the rest of the call.
Step 05 — Dig Into the Process
Got it. Walk me through the process you use to do that.
You are mapping how leads, sales, and revenue actually flow. Listen for repetitive steps, handoffs, and manual work. Those are the AI opportunities.
Bottleneck question (if needed):
Okay, so what takes up the most time in that process, or what's the biggest bottleneck keeping you from doing more of it?
Now you have two things: what works and what's in the way. That combination is where AI lives.
Steps 6–8

Suggest, Compare, Connect

Step 06 — Suggest a Loose AI Fit
Okay, cool. Based on what you're telling me, I do believe AI can help you here. For example, [describe the fit in one or two sentences]. We did something similar for [client or vertical] and the result was [quick outcome].

That could certainly be something worth talking about in a longer conversation.
Don't pitch a full solution. Keep the case study short. One sentence what they did, one sentence what it produced.
Step 07 — Ask About Competitors
Let me ask you this: what are other companies in your same space, in your same niche, doing that you see working for them? What do you see your most successful competitors doing?
Then the key qualifying question: Are you doing those things?
If not — you've found a proven, market-validated play they're not running. That is a strong AI fit.
Step 08 — Connect AI to the Proven Plays
Based on what you're describing, there are proven messages and systems already working for companies like yours. AI and automation can help you replicate those without needing to hire a bigger team or add hours to your week.
Steps 9–10

The Ask & Hand Off

Step 09 — Soft Recommendation
I'll be honest with you. Based on what you've shared, I genuinely believe AI can help you in a couple of specific ways, [name one or two]. If I didn't think that, I wouldn't say so. Do you have time this week for a short video call to talk through it properly?
Stay quiet after the ask. Let them answer. If they say yes, move to scheduling. If they hesitate, treat it as a soft smokescreen and go to Part 3.
Step 10 — Confirm and Hand Off to Kyle
Perfect. I'll have Kyle, who leads the AI side, on that call with us. I'm sending the invite to [email] right now, you should see it hit your inbox in the next minute or two.
📅
Lock a specific day and time window. Not "later this week."
📧
Confirm the email address for the calendar invite.
👤
Tell them Kyle will be on the call as the AI lead.
⚡
Send the invite immediately while still on the phone, if possible.
Guiding Principles

Rules for the Caller

❓
Ask, don't pitch. Your questions should outnumber your statements at least three to one.
🎯
Anchor every AI suggestion to something they just told you. No generic promises.
💬
Use the word "genuinely" sparingly and mean it. The honesty framing is a pattern break from typical cold calls.
🔊
Never oversell on the phone. Your job is to earn the video call, not the deal.
🔇
Pause after questions. Silence is a qualifier, not a problem.
📝
Take notes in real time. Kyle needs the "what's working," "what's the bottleneck," and "competitor moves" answers for the close call.
Part 2: Mindset

A Smokescreen Is Not an Objection

Smokescreen

Reflexive. Happens in the first 15 seconds, before they've heard anything. It is not a real assessment of your offer. It is the autopilot response a business owner gives when they suspect they're talking to a salesperson.

Real Objection

A thoughtful reason someone doesn't want what you're offering. It comes after they understand what you do. Real objections deserve real answers.

If you treat a smokescreen like a real objection, you will try to overcome it with logic or features, and you will lose. You cannot argue someone into wanting to talk to you.
The move: Acknowledge the smokescreen as valid, show you're not what they expected, and redirect the conversation back to them. Once they're talking about their business instead of fending off a pitch, the smokescreen evaporates on its own.
The Universal Pattern

Acknowledge. Bridge. Pivot.

Every smokescreen response in Part 3 follows this exact pattern.

01
Acknowledge
Genuinely, briefly. Do not apologize. Validate what they said.
02
Bridge
Lower the stakes. Make it clear you're not here to pitch.
03
Pivot
Ask a short, easy, business-focused question that puts them back in the driver's seat.
Memorize this pattern before you memorize any script.
Smokescreen 1
01 "I'm busy right now."
Why they say it: This is the default. 90% of owners will say some version of this, whether they're actually busy or not. It is a reflex.
Acknowledge
Yeah, I totally get it. That's what it is to be a business owner, there's always something else to handle.
Bridge
My job isn't to give you a full spiel about how AI can help your business today, right now. It's just to see if a longer conversation is going to be worthwhile.
Pivot
Would you say there are any bottlenecks right now that you suspect automation, better systems, or AI could help you with?
Branch A: They Engage
They name a bottleneck. Proceed as if the smokescreen never happened. Continue into the clean-path flow at Step 5.
Branch B: Still No Time
Don't push. Ask: "When would be a better time? I just need ten minutes over the phone." Lock it and count it as a win.
Smokescreen 2
02 "AI is slop."
Why they say it: They tried an AI tool, got bad output, and wrote off the whole category. Or pure skepticism from articles / social media.
Acknowledge (Agree Partially)
Yeah, AI is a lot like training a human employee. It typically doesn't get it right the first time. There's an artful way to use it, and most people never get past the first bad output.
Bridge (With Conviction)
What I can guarantee is this: the companies that leverage AI at a high level are going to vastly outperform the ones that don't. [Give a quick, specific example.]
Pivot
That being said, I don't want to diagnose and tell you what you need to do for your business, because I don't know your situation. Do you mind telling me what you want to get done and where you'd like to see your company be in the next year or so?
After they share a goal, ask: "Got it. What's keeping you from getting there right now?" — Now you have a goal and an obstacle. Return to clean-path flow at Step 6 or 9.
Smokescreen 3
03 "I can't afford to hire anyone right now."
Why they say it: They assume your offer looks like a traditional agency retainer or a full-time hire. They're trying to disqualify themselves before you get to pricing.
Acknowledge & Disarm
Yeah, and honestly, if that's the case, I'd be more than happy to point you toward AI tools you can use at a very low cost until it makes sense to work with someone like me.
Bridge
That being said, do you mind if I ask a couple of questions about your business to see where you're at, so I can point you in the right direction?
Pivot (Qualify Gently)
Roughly how much are you doing in monthly revenue? What's bringing in the most business? Where do things fall apart or slow down?
Branch A: They Qualify
Pivot back to real conversation. "Based on what you're sharing, I genuinely think we can help. We start small and build. Would a video call this week make sense?"
Branch B: They Don't Qualify
Deliver on the promise. Give them 1-2 genuine low-cost AI tool suggestions. Thank them warmly. Reps who deliver free pointers build referral networks by accident.
Smokescreen 4
04 "Send me an email with some information."
Why they say it: The polite brush-off. They don't trust you enough yet to spend real time. A generic email they skim and delete costs them nothing.
Acknowledge
Yeah, happy to, and I absolutely will.
Bridge
Honestly though, a generic email isn't going to be nearly as useful as a two-minute conversation, because then I can actually send you something that fits your situation instead of a boilerplate pitch.
Pivot
So real quick, what would you actually want that email to help you with? What's top of mind for you on the business right now?
Branch A: They Answer
The email request evaporates. They're in the conversation. Proceed into Step 5 of the clean-path flow.
Branch B: Still Want Email
Qualify the email: "So I send something worth your time, what's the one thing you'd want it to address?" Whatever they say is your hook for the follow-up.
Smokescreen 5
05 "Is this a sales call?"
Why they say it: They're testing you. They've been burned by callers who dodge the question or lie. Total honesty earns more credibility in 3 seconds than most reps earn in the whole call.
Acknowledge (Total Honesty)
Yeah, it is, technically. I'm calling because I think I can actually help.
Bridge
That said, my job isn't to pitch you today. It's to see if a longer conversation is worth your time. If it's not, I'll tell you that, and we both save ourselves the effort.
Pivot
So let me ask, what's one area of the business right now where, if you had a magic wand, you'd want more leads, more time back, or a better system?
Do not soften this. Do not dodge. Reps who say "well, not really, it's more of a consultation" get hung up on immediately because they sound evasive. The confidence in the honesty is the move.
Smokescreen 6
06 "I'm not interested."
Why they say it: The nuclear smokescreen. Usually comes in the first 10 seconds, before they've actually heard anything. They can't be "not interested" because they have no idea what you're offering. It's pure reflex.
Acknowledge
Totally fair, I haven't given you any reason to be.
Bridge
Can I ask you one quick question, and then I'll let you go either way?
Pivot
If there was one thing in your business right now that's eating up too much of your time or leaving money on the table, what would it be?
Branch A: They Engage (~1 in 3)
You're back in. Do NOT break the "one question" promise by launching into a pitch. Let the conversation develop naturally for another minute before proposing anything.
Branch B: Still No
Honor the promise. Thank them, end warmly, move on. The rep who respects the exit here is the rep the owner doesn't block from calling back in 6 months.
Smokescreen 7
07 "Just tell me how much it costs."
Why they say it: Price-first is avoidance disguised as directness. A number kills the call either way: too high and you're out, too low and you've commoditized yourself before they understand what they're buying.
Acknowledge
Yeah, totally fair question, and I respect you wanting to cut to it.
Bridge
The honest answer is, pricing depends entirely on what would actually move the needle for you, and I genuinely can't tell you that until I know a little about your situation. Throwing out a random number right now would either scare you off for no reason or undersell what you actually need.
Pivot
Can I ask sixty seconds of questions to figure out what you'd actually need, and then I'll give you a real answer on price?
Branch A: They Agree
Ask qualifying questions (revenue, team, what's working, bottleneck). Then give a range anchored to value: "This kind of engagement is usually in the X to Y range."
Branch B: Push for Number
Give a range: "Typical engagements land from [low] to [high] per month. The range is wide because two businesses in the same industry need different things."

Rules to Call By

Sound Like a Consultant,
Not a Telemarketer.

Acknowledge before you bridge. Acknowledge before you bridge. Acknowledge before you bridge.
Never argue with a smokescreen. Agree with it, lower the stakes, then pivot.
The pivot is always a short, easy, business-focused question.
If they won't engage after one pivot, ask for ten minutes on their calendar. A scheduled call counts as a win.
If they truly won't qualify, deliver on the free pointer. Referrals come from the calls you didn't close.
Your job is to earn the video call with Kyle, not close the deal on the phone.
